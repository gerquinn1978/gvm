The world we're in

Vibe coding is fast — and that is the only promise it makes.

Karpathy coined the term in 2025. Natural language, conversational flow, low barrier. The feedback loop is the fastest in the history of software development.

Don't defend vibe coding or attack it. Start from its actual value: speed. The audience uses it. The problem is not speed.
The problem

AI produces plausible output. Plausible is not the same as correct.

The code compiles. The obvious tests pass. The PR looks fine on a fast read. The failures arrive in production — edge cases, security assumptions, integration boundaries wired incorrectly.

This is the transition from speed to quality. The audience has felt this. Don't argue for it — just name it. They know.

AI-assisted development hits two failure modes — and it's structurally good at both.

Build the wrong thing  •  Build the right thing badly

Brooks: deciding precisely what to build is the hardest part, and the one whose mistakes most cripple the result. The second failure mode is what Beck, Fowler, Freeman & Pryce address. AI is compliant — it will produce something for any prompt, whether the prompt points at the right target or not.

One sentence activates thousands of pages of expert knowledge.

This compression extends to every expert in the roster.

The core mechanism in one diagram. Thousands of pages, absorbed during training, activated by a single sentence. Without the citation, the model draws from the broad distribution. With it, it shifts to the specific framework. This is citation as retrieval key.

GVM organises expertise into three tiers by scope.

Three tiers, not one expert. Tier 1 always active — they govern the process. Tier 2 conditional — they activate on domain signals. Tier 3 after tech choices. The key point: every tier cites published works, not abstract roles.

Named experts replace vague roles at every pipeline phase.

  • Requirements — Wiegers, Gause & Weinberg, Patton, Cooper, Christensen
  • Test cases — Copeland, Beizer, North (BDD), Kaner
  • Architecture — Clements, Brown, Fairbanks, Keeling, Brooks
  • Code review — four parallel panels by defect class
  • Documentation — Doumont, Zinsser, Redish, Orwell

"Architect" is not an expert. Fairbanks, Brooks, and Keeling are.

The difference between "an expert" and a named person with a book matters. "Act as an architect" activates a blend of everything. "Apply Fairbanks's risk-driven depth from Just Enough Architecture" activates a specific framework. The specificity is the mechanism.

The pipeline runs from outcome to release — with feedback at every seam.

14 phases. Four groups: discover, construct, verify, release. The red dashed arrows are the feedback loops — downstream failures routing issues back upstream. This is not waterfall; the arrows flow both ways by design.

Every phase asks: is this the best this expert's framework can produce?

The published authorities in requirements, testing, architecture, and security are present in the team — activated by citation, on demand, with the depth of their original works.

This is the sparkline pivot — from "what is" (vibe coding ceiling) to "what could be" (GVM). The dark slide marks the turn. Keep it to one sentence. Let the contrast do the work.
Key structural principle

Test cases come before architecture. If a requirement can't be expressed as a test, the requirement is ambiguous.

This gate catches vagueness at the cheapest point — before any design work begins. AI is compliant: ask it to implement an ambiguous requirement and it will produce something, confidently, and wrong in ways that are hard to detect.

The test-first gate is the most surprising thing practitioners hear. Flip it on them: "when did you last have a requirement that couldn't be tested?" The silence is the problem. Test cases as a quality gate on requirements, not as a downstream activity.

Parallel expert panels catch what a single reviewer misses.

Four panels, concurrent, non-overlapping mandates. Satisfaction-of-search bias means a single reviewer who finds one defect is less likely to find others nearby. Separate mandates fix this. The Laitenberger finding is from the whitepaper — cite it accurately.

Progressive calibration means every review round is more precise than the last.

The calibration file is the memory the AI doesn't have between sessions. Without it, round 10 is no more informed than round 1. With it, the reviewer knows which modules have recurring findings and which are clean. Fagan's principle: fix major defects and the minor ones become visible.

MVP-1 forces the first build chunk through every external boundary.

The natural AI tendency is horizontal — build the DB layer, then service, then API, then UI. Each layer is internally coherent so the AI is confident. Integration risk accumulates silently until the last layer lands and then it's expensive. MVP-1 forces the first slice vertical — thin but end-to-end.
Release gate

The release gate refuses to tag unless every review verdict is resolved.

Ship-ready / Demo-ready / Not shippable. Stubs that surface to the UI block Ship-ready. The verdict is mechanical, not advisory. A quality system that does not enforce its verdicts at release is a quality aspiration.

The difference between a quality system and a quality aspiration. If the final step does not check the preceding steps, those steps are suggestions. The release gate makes them structural.

Grounded output scores measurably higher — and ungrounded review cannot see the difference.

The slope chart shows the reversal in one glance. In Exp A (ungrounded review), G1 ungrounded appears at the top — the ungrounded reviewer cannot distinguish it. Experiment result

The improvements were real — but invisible without the framework to evaluate them.

Expert-grounded review confirmed enormous effects: chartjunk absence d = 3.67, data-ink ratio d = 3.87, chart type appropriateness d = 2.27. Cohen's d = 0.8 is conventionally "large." Values above 2 are exceptional.

Ungrounded reviewers scored the same outputs without seeing the difference. The experts' published works are the definition of good. Only reviewers who know those standards can see the convergence.

The effect sizes are real numbers from the whitepaper. Don't round them or invent them. The point: these are not marginal differences. The improvement was large by any conventional measure — and invisible to reviewers who lacked the expert framework.

The dose-response pattern reproduced identically across all three model families.

Model family Exp A ranking (ungrounded review) Exp B ranking (expert review) Reversal?
Claude Opus 4.6 No predicted order G1 < G2 < G3 Yes
GPT-5.4 No predicted order G1 < G2 < G3 Yes
Gemini 3.1 Pro No predicted order G1 < G2 < G3 Yes

Ranking reversal is not a property of any single model's training. It is a general property of how large language models respond to citation-based expert grounding.

Limitation: same-model evaluation (each model reviewed its own output). Cross-model review remains open work.

This is the table slide — precise values are the point. The audience needs to see the three-row claim. The limitation is disclosed honestly — same-model evaluation is the remaining threat. The whitepaper is explicit about this; so should the presentation be.

Ungrounded evaluation is blind to what it helped create.

To evaluate quality, you need the same frameworks that produced quality. An ungrounded reviewer cannot distinguish well-designed output from plausible output — because distinguishing them requires Tufte's data-ink ratio or Beizer's boundary value analysis or whatever expert the producer cited.

This is the epistemological punchline. Both halves require grounding. The experiment showed both sides: Exp A (ungrounded review couldn't see it) and Exp B (expert review revealed the full ordering). Neither half alone is the finding — the comparison between them is.

The plugin is built using GVM

GVM was built using GVM — the plugin is its own case study.

The methodology was applied end-to-end on the plugin itself: requirements, test cases, tech spec, design review, walking skeleton, build, code review, test, doc write, release.

Not a controlled experiment — but it is a real project with real artefacts and real findings. The most instructive one: expert grounding during code construction did not prevent defects that expert grounding during review caught immediately.

The plugin case study is in the whitepaper section 9.1. The honest disclosure matters here: the limitation (grounding may be less effective during code construction than during review) was discovered through dogfooding, not predicted. That is the point — methodologies that aren't tested against real builds can't discover this class of failure.

For practitioners

Five things change when you adopt GVM.

  • Citations replace generic prompts — "apply Beizer's boundary value analysis" instead of "write good tests"
  • Test cases come before architecture — requirements must prove testability first
  • Reviews produce verdicts, not suggestions — findings are triaged, not silently bypassed
  • The calibration file accumulates across rounds — by round 3, you have project-specific context
  • Build checks enforce what reviews found — past defects become prevention gates
These are changes practitioners feel directly. The most surprising: test cases before architecture. The most impactful over time: the calibration file accumulating. Lead with the concrete behaviour change, not the principle behind it.
For leaders

Three decisions that cannot be delegated to the team.

  • Your Critical-finding policy under deadline pressure — write it down before the first deadline conflict. "Defer with rationale" is acceptable; "ignore because we're busy" is not.
  • The expert roster for your primary domain — the default covers general software. Finance, healthcare, regulated industries need domain-specific panels. One session, done once, compounds across every subsequent review.
  • Whether your team is actually reading AI-generated code — if not, GVM structures and amplifies a practice that isn't happening. Fix the practice first.
Leaders need to know the three decisions that are specifically theirs. The first is a pre-commitment problem — without a written policy, the decision gets made under pressure, and under pressure, gates erode. The third is the meta-question: GVM amplifies review discipline; it cannot create it.

Start with doc review. It costs nothing and teaches everything the gate requires.

Run /gvm-doc-review and /gvm-doc-write on internal documents for four to six weeks. No pipeline change. No SDLC disruption. The team experiences what "finding triaged" means and what a verdict looks like — before those concepts apply to production code.

This is the action slide — the start of the "what could be" zone. The contrast slide marks that this is now instruction, not description. Standalone doc review is the recommended entry point precisely because it is bounded, low-stakes, and teaches the review discipline cheaply.

Three adoption paths, in order of commitment required.

  • Option 1 — Standalone first (recommended) — doc review only, 4–6 weeks, no pipeline change. Teaches the gate before it touches code.
  • Option 2 — Toe in water — one team, one greenfield project, full pipeline. 6–10 weeks. Protect the scope or deadline pressure will contaminate the signal.
  • Option 3 — Phase by phase — requirements and test cases first; add build and review once the team trusts the upstream artefacts. Slowest path, stickiest adoption.

All three are legitimate. The right one depends on your risk tolerance and how much process change the team can absorb at once.

No recommendation among the three — the leader in the room has to decide. The note about protecting scope in Option 2 is real: if a pilot project is under delivery pressure, the pipeline becomes the first thing cut. That poisons the signal.

The expert roster grows through use, not maintenance.

The roster grows with use rather than requiring centralised maintenance. Tier 2b specifically: the market risk reference file built for one trading system serves the next. Each project deepens the file. This is the compound interest of expert knowledge.

Citation logging creates the technical precondition for expert compensation.

The attribution argument is not the core of the methodology, but it's a consequence that matters. Today, experts whose frameworks are activated thousands of times see no compensation. GVM's citation log creates the technical precondition. The compensation infrastructure (collecting society model) is open work — the paper names it as such.
Important clarification

GVM is a methodology. Installing the plugin is not adopting GVM.

The plugin implements the methodology; the team's process is what enforces the quality gate. GVM makes structured review a gate the team enforces on every meaningful change — not a step they skip when the deadline presses. If they aren't doing that, the plugin cannot do it for them.

This is the key message for leaders specifically. The technology is easy to install. The process change is not. Leaders who install the plugin without establishing the review discipline will get artefacts but not quality gates.

Calibrated vs blind score gap How much the calibration file adds Gap > 1.5 pts: check for staleness
Finding count by expert panel Where defects cluster > 2 Critical/round: schedule improvement session
Review rounds to passing verdict How much rework each change requires Average > 3: look upstream at requirements quality
Rework rate after code review Whether the gate is catching things Zero rework on 3 consecutive reviews: audit for silent suppression

Common failure modes

These failures have visible signals in the artefacts — if you know where to look.

  • Silent suppression — findings marked "N/A" with no rationale in calibration.md. Check the N/A entries monthly.
  • Batched verdicts — verdict recorded before all findings are triaged. A shared-rule violation, typically under deadline pressure.
  • Skipping calibration — treating first-pass review scores as decision-grade. Three calibrated reviews minimum before scores mean anything.
  • Small-change bypass — "this change is too small for a review gate." Define the threshold before the first temptation, not during it.
From the engineering leaders guide. The most dangerous combination: silent suppression + deadline pressure + small-change bypass. Each is individually manageable; the combination unwinds the methodology quickly. The calibration file is the early warning system.
Scope

The full pipeline is not always warranted — but the citation is always free.

For throwaway scripts, rapid prototypes, or CRUD applications with no novel components, the pipeline structure may exceed its benefit. Even there, one sentence costs nothing: "apply Kleppmann's cache-aside pattern" activates a body of work whether or not you are running the full pipeline.

Important honesty slide. Don't oversell the pipeline for every project. The citation mechanism scales down as well as up. The overhead is the pipeline; the citation is essentially free. Let the audience calibrate this for their context.
Your first step

  • Teams — /gvm-requirements on the next feature. One requirement at a time.
