Expert-anchored AI development — from requirements to release

GVM builds a development process around citation-based expert activation.

The Grounded Vibe Methodology keeps what works about vibe coding — natural language, low barrier to entry, conversational flow — while anchoring every phase to named experts and their published works.

  • Adzic, Cagan, and Patton ground the discovery work — deciding what is worth building.
  • Beck, Fagan, Freeman & Pryce, and Beizer ground the construction and verification work — building it correctly.
  • Every discipline is guided by named authorities with specific published works, not generic role labels.
Atkinson: one idea per slide. The one idea here is "GVM = citation-based grounding + structured process." Don't wander into the pipeline yet.

Six principles distinguish GVM from adjacent approaches.

  • Named Experts, Not Abstract Personas — every discipline uses named authorities with specific published works.
  • Multi-Expert Panels — 3–7 experts per discipline whose competing frameworks force principled trade-offs.
  • Conditional Activation — specialists load only when their domain appears, keeping context focused.
  • Curated Reference Files — expert knowledge codified in phase-keyed files structured for maximum citation effect.
  • Self-Improving Expert Roster — the roster grows through use via discovery and divergence analysis.
  • Builds on Published Canon — GVM activates established methodologies; it does not replace them.
Source: whitepaper Section 1, Design Principles block. These are the stated principles from the paper — do not embellish.

Expertise activates in three tiers, each with a different scope and trigger.

Source: whitepaper Section 4. The three tiers are the load-bearing structure. Tier 2 splits into 2a (software domains — ships with GVM) and 2b (industry domains — curated per project). Don't conflate them.

Panels of competing experts force principled trade-offs that single experts cannot.

The architecture panel illustrates the dynamic. Fairbanks says "specify only the risky parts." Brooks says "maintain conceptual integrity everywhere." Neither wins outright.

The panel's resolution for a low-risk utility module: a light specification (Fairbanks) that explicitly cross-references the system's naming conventions, error handling, and logging format (Brooks). Brief but connected to the whole.

This is not additive. It is a genuine trade-off where competing priorities produce a result neither expert would reach alone.

Source: whitepaper Section 3.4. Use the concrete configuration-loader example if asked. Don't claim this is the only benefit of panels — panel size (3–7) is also calibrated to avoid expert overload, which is a documented failure mode.

The citation acts as a retrieval key into the model's trained knowledge.

Large language models process text that references major works during pre-training. These associations are encoded in the model's attention patterns.

When a prompt names "Beizer" alongside "boundary value," the model's attention activates representations associated with Beizer's specific formulation: the six-value boundary test and the distinction between on-point and off-point testing.

Without the citation, the model produces a blend — typically a three-value test representing multiple sources averaged together.

The retrieval-key mechanism is the working hypothesis the methodology is built on. The experimental evidence in Section 7 of the whitepaper demonstrates the quality effect rather than directly testing the mechanism.

Be careful here — the whitepaper is explicit that the retrieval-key mechanism is a working hypothesis, supported by transformer interpretability research but not directly tested by the dashboard experiment. Don't overclaim.

GVM differs from role prompting, SDD, BMAD, and RAG on one organising axis.

The whitepaper is explicit: this is a descriptive structural comparison against published documentation. There is no head-to-head controlled experiment comparing GVM to these approaches. Don't imply otherwise.

Ungrounded evaluation cannot see the improvement that grounded output produces.

The controlled experiment revealed not one finding but two: grounding improves quality, and you need the experts' own frameworks to see that it has. Without the framework, the improvement is invisible.

Duarte: the contrast slide marks the pivot in the sparkline. This is the "why it matters" moment — not just that GVM produces better output, but that you cannot detect the improvement without grounding the evaluation too. This is the single sentence the audience should leave with.

A controlled experiment across three model families confirmed the quality effect.

180 market risk dashboards generated under three conditions: ungrounded, design-grounded (Tufte & Few), and fully grounded (design + domain + writing experts). Shuffled, anonymised, reviewed blind.

1,080 blind reviews total: 180 dashboards × 3 reviewers × 2 review instruments, reproduced across Claude, GPT, and Gemini.

Two review instruments — one derived from ungrounded AI consensus, one from the cited experts' published principles.

Full design, per-dimension scores, and statistical analysis in the companion paper Grounding Produces Measurably Better Output.

Source: whitepaper Section 7.1. Don't give statistics without context — always lead with the experimental design. The p-value (p < 0.001) and effect sizes (Cohen's d up to 3.87) are in the next slide.

The group ranking reversed between the ungrounded and expert-grounded review instruments.

Source: whitepaper Section 7.2. The pattern reproduced identically across Claude Opus 4.6, GPT-5.4, and Gemini 3.1 Pro. Same-model evaluation remains a limitation — cross-model review has not been performed. Be explicit about this limitation if asked.

Evaluating quality requires the same frameworks that producing quality requires.

The Group 2 improvements were real — expert reviewers confirmed enormous effect sizes on chartjunk absence, data-ink ratio, and chart type appropriateness. But ungrounded reviewers could not see them. The improvements were invisible without the framework to evaluate them.

This is the core claim made concrete: ungrounded AI does not know what "good" looks like, because it lacks a framework for evaluating quality. The experts' published works are the definition of good. Grounded output converges toward those standards. Only reviewers who know those standards can detect the convergence.

Source: whitepaper Section 7.3. This slide answers "so what?" for the experiment finding. Don't rush past it.

GVM addresses two distinct AI failure modes that unstructured vibe coding hits reliably.

Source: whitepaper Sections 1 and 5. Brooks named failure mode 1 in 1975. Both failure modes pass casual inspection. Both can ship. GVM's phase set is structured so neither has a path to the release tag.

GVM reduces hallucination's surface area structurally, not by eliminating it.

AI-generated code contains security vulnerabilities at rates around 40% (Pearce et al., 2022). Around 21.7% of open-source model package recommendations point to libraries that do not exist (Spracklen et al., 2024). No technique eliminates hallucination entirely.

GVM's structural defences:

  • Artefact contracts — structured IDs and traceability links are harder to hallucinate than vague prose.
  • Strict TDD — catches hallucinated API methods immediately.
  • Parallel review panels — hallucinated findings are unlikely to appear consistently across independent panels.
  • Independent source verification — a separate agent confirms that cited experts and publications exist.
Source: whitepaper Section 5.8. The statistics (40%, 21.7%) are cited with sources in the whitepaper. Don't quote them without attribution.

Making expert citations visible creates the technical precondition for expert compensation.

AI models already contain the full body of work from the experts GVM names. Practitioners who previously would have bought books now have that knowledge through AI — often without awareness of its provenance.

GVM logs two events for every expert: loaded (reference file read into context) and cited (named as the source of a decision in output). That log is the technical precondition for a compensation framework.

The parallel to streaming music royalties is instructive: compensation eventually arrived through per-play licensing infrastructure, not enforcement. The structural question is identical: how does value generated by widespread activation flow back to the creators of the activated knowledge?

Source: whitepaper Sections 6.4 and 8. This is not a solved problem — GVM creates the precondition. The compensation infrastructure does not yet exist. Don't overstate GVM's role here.

A phased pipeline activates the right experts at each stage.

Source: whitepaper Section 5. The pipeline is not a waterfall — the feedback arrows are the methodology working as designed. Don't present it as rigid top-to-bottom.

Discovery phases ensure every requirement traces to an actor outcome before any code is written.

/gvm-impact-map produces a four-level goal → actor → impact → deliverable map (Adzic). No requirement may finalise unless it traces to a leaf deliverable on this map.

/gvm-requirements elicits, structures, and prioritises requirements through expert conversation — Wiegers on classification, Patton on user story mapping, Gause & Weinberg on questioning techniques.

/gvm-test-cases generates acceptance tests before the technical specification — a quality gate on requirements, not a downstream activity. If a requirement cannot be expressed as a concrete test, it is not yet ready.

Source: whitepaper Section 5.1 and 5.3. The test-first ordering is deliberate and structural. Beizer, Copeland, North, and Kaner ground the test case generation. By the time the spec begins, every requirement has been validated through testability.

The first build chunk must touch every external boundary the system will ever cross.

This is MVP-1 ordering (Cockburn, Reinertsen, Freeman & Pryce): the first chunk produces a thin vertical slice through process, network, persistence, and third-party boundaries. It does almost nothing functionally. It proves the wiring works.

Subsequent chunks add behaviour through boundaries that are already exercised. Integration risk is bounded by what the first slice validated, not discovered at the end when assumptions have hardened.

MVP-1 is enforced mechanically at three points: the tech spec chunk plan, the build phase hard gate, and the design review's final pass.

Source: whitepaper Section 5.13. Four chunk shapes are exempt: library, refactor, performance-driven, fully-specified. An exempted chunk declares its category and rationale in its handover — the rationale is the audit trail.

Three tiers of experts activate at different pipeline phases.

Parallel non-overlapping review panels catch what a single reviewer misses.

Grounded in Laitenberger et al. (Perspective-Based Reading, 2000): non-overlapping mandates detected 41% more unique defects at 58% lower cost per defect. Four panels execute in one review's wall-clock time.

Source: whitepaper Section 5.6. Satisfaction-of-search bias (Cain, Adamo & Mitroff, 2013; Drew, Võ & Wolfe, 2013) is why a single reviewer misses things. Liberal detection threshold on first pass — Signal Detection Theory (Green & Swets, 1966). Synthesis filters false positives.

Reviews calibrate across rounds — and correct for the blind spots calibration creates.

The AI review tools treat each round as independent. GVM accumulates project-specific knowledge: score histories per dimension, anchor examples from this project, recurring findings tracked until resolved.

By round 3, the reviewer knows which modules score well, which have recurring issues, and what has already been flagged twice. Reviews converge toward the actual quality profile rather than rediscovering the same issues each round.

But calibration introduces anchoring bias. From round 3 onwards, a blind reviewer runs in parallel — same criteria, same experts, no calibration data. Reconciliation merges genuinely new findings; discards rediscovered noise. The practitioner sees one report, not two.

Source: whitepaper Section 5.7. Grounded in Fagan's inspection methodology, Shewhart's common-cause vs special-cause distinction, and Tversky & Kahneman's anchoring bias research. Promoted build checks (permanent vs retirable) are the memory system — don't skip them.

Not every citation carries equal weight — a scoring system prevents self-serving assessments.

Two independent scales: one for the expert (Authority, Publication, Breadth, Adoption, Currency), one for each work (Specificity, Depth, Currency, Influence). Classifications from Canonical to Provisional.

Two safeguards prevent the AI from scoring its own citations unchecked:

  • Evidence requirement — any score of 4 or 5 must include verifiable evidence the practitioner can check independently.
  • Independent agent review — a separate agent, with no access to the original scores, produces its own assessment. Convergence builds confidence; divergence is flagged for the practitioner to decide.
Source: whitepaper Sections 4.4 and Appendix A. The scoring mirrors model validation practice in financial services (SR 11-7): the validator must be independent of the developer. Classifications: Canonical ≥4.5, Established 3.5–4.4, Recognised 2.5–3.4, Emerging 1.5–2.4, Provisional below 1.5.

The expert roster grows through use, not centralised maintenance.

When a domain is encountered with no matching specialist, the AI identifies the gap, researches candidate experts with published work, scores them using independent verification, and presents the candidates to the practitioner. Experts who pass verification are added permanently.

Tier 2b (industry domain) files compound value across projects. The market risk reference file built for one trading system serves the next, deepening with each project rather than starting fresh. A financial services practitioner running five projects accumulates a richer expert panel than one built in a single session.

Source: whitepaper Sections 4.5 and 4.6. Discovery applies the same independent verification as initial scoring — the most dangerous failure mode is confidently wrong citations entering the roster unchecked.

Expert grounding is demonstrably effective during design and review — its effectiveness during code construction is undemonstrated.

The code review caught issues that expert-grounded build prompts should have prevented: a DRY violation that Hunt & Thomas grounding should have stopped, but did not. The same violation was caught immediately by the independent review phase, grounded in the same experts.

The correction: the build prompt now separates a Framework Reference (procedural knowledge — API docs, method signatures) from Review Criteria (declarative knowledge — expert principles applied in the code-review loop, not during construction).

Evidence from one project, no control group. The separation is implemented; whether it improves construction quality is itself an open question.

Source: whitepaper Section 9.1. This is an honest limitation — don't downplay it. The whitepaper is explicit: "evidence is compatible with multiple explanations." The declarative/procedural hypothesis is the load-bearing assumption; it has not been independently validated.

GVM scales down as well as up — the citation mechanism itself costs nothing.

The full pipeline is designed for production systems where quality matters: regulated industries, software people depend on, systems where the cost of defects is high. It is not designed for throwaway scripts or weekend prototypes.

But the citation mechanism itself — without the full pipeline — costs one sentence: "Apply Kleppmann's cache-aside pattern with TTLs calibrated to data volatility." That sentence activates a body of work whether or not you are running the full GVM pipeline.

GVM's overhead is also front-loaded. Once the expert reference files are assembled, the per-project cost is low. The same files serve every subsequent project in that domain.

Source: whitepaper Sections 1 (last paragraph) and 5.4. Don't oversell — acknowledge the setup cost is real. But also don't undersell the minimum-viable form, which is just citation-based grounding without any pipeline machinery.

Existing codebases get a diagnostic entry point that routes to the right pipeline phase.

The site survey phase has the AI explore the codebase, diagnose its architectural state across multiple health dimensions, select the appropriate grounding experts, and recommend where to enter the pipeline.

A codebase that scores high on coherence but low on currency gets different expert selection than one that scores low across the board. Purely technical remediation can skip directly to specification — the survey has defined what the codebase is, what it should be, and where the gaps are.

The grounded experts become a lens for recovering lost rationale and identifying design drift, not just a guide for extending the system.

Source: whitepaper Section 5.5. This matters for the leadership audience — most of them are managing existing codebases, not greenfield projects. Don't skip this slide.

GVM is a methodology, not a product — it travels as structured text, not tooling.

The components — expert panels, curated reference files, phased pipelines, conditional activation — are patterns implementable in any AI development environment that supports contextual instructions.

What travels directly: expert reference files (Markdown), pipeline phase definitions, scoring rubrics, build-check format.

What requires per-tool adaptation: the conditional activation mechanism, parallel panel dispatch, and calibration file location.

The reference implementation uses Claude Code. A port to Cursor replaces skill invocation with rules and chat hooks while keeping the reference files unchanged.

Source: whitepaper Section 6.1. The tool-agnostic claim is structural, not experimental. It has not been ported to Cursor or GitHub Copilot. Be precise about what "tool-agnostic" does and does not mean.

The Assay project demonstrates GVM applied end-to-end on a production tool.

Assay is a data-quality analysis tool built using GVM. The case study covers the full pipeline: impact mapping through deployment, with the methodology's own review scoring system providing the measurement instrument.

Key findings from the case study:

  • The build-check system learned from its own history — recurring findings were promoted to permanent checks, preventing reintroduction of the same defect classes.
  • The MVP-1 slice exposed integration gaps in session 1 that would otherwise have appeared in the final integration phase.
  • The code-review loop (four parallel panels) caught the DRY violation that motivated the build-prompt separation now in the methodology.
Source: companion paper "Building a Production Tool with GVM" and case-study-assay.html. The case study is a single-project, no-control-group evaluation. It is illustrative, not experimental. Don't present it as equivalent to the dashboard experiment.

Three experimental questions remain open.

  • Cross-model review — the experiment used same-model evaluation (each model reviewing its own output). Cross-model review — Claude reviewing Gemini output — has not been performed.
  • Expert grounding during construction — whether separating Framework Reference from Review Criteria improves construction quality is measurable but unmeasured. The methodology's review scoring system provides the instrument; the study has not been run.
  • Discovery axis effectiveness — the experiment validated the construction axis (dashboard quality). The discovery axis — impact mapping, traceability gates, exploratory testing effectiveness — requires separate experimental validation.
Source: whitepaper Sections 7.4 and 9. Showing open questions builds credibility. Don't hide them to make GVM look more complete than it is.

The minimum-viable starting point is one citation in your next prompt.

You do not need the full pipeline to get the grounding effect. Name the expert, cite the work, specify the technique. Do this on the next task where quality matters.

  • For practitioners: add one expert citation to your next prompt. Compare the output to the ungrounded version of the same task. The difference is immediate and visible.
  • For teams considering the full pipeline: run /gvm-site-survey on an existing codebase. The diagnostic report shows where expert grounding would change what the AI produces — before committing to the setup cost.
  • For leadership: the controlled experiment is in the whitepaper. The open questions are named. Ask your team to run a trial on one project and measure output quality before and after.
Doumont: close with one concrete next step the audience can take. The action slide is type-specific to "Inform / explain" — it replaces the persuade type's "single ask." Give the audience something to do tomorrow morning, not just something to think about.

Three things to take away.

  • Citations work as retrieval keys. Naming a specific expert and a specific work shifts AI output from a broad blend to a focused application of that expert's framework. A controlled experiment across three model families confirms the quality effect (p < 0.001).
  • You need the framework to see the improvement. Ungrounded reviewers cannot detect what grounded output produces. Evaluating quality requires the same expert frameworks that producing quality requires.
  • The methodology addresses both failure modes. Building the wrong thing fluently, and building the right thing badly. Both pass casual inspection. GVM's phase set gates both structurally, not as guidelines.
