Grounded Vibe Methodology

Citation-based grounding for AI-assisted software development

Road-map slide. Keep it brief — the audience needs a thread, not a summary.

AI-assisted development is no longer experimental — it is how software gets written.

Vibe coding (Karpathy, 2025) — natural language, conversational flow, low barrier to entry — is real and it works. The question is whether it produces software that holds up.

Frame the context. "Vibe coding" is Andrej Karpathy's term. Acknowledge what it gets right before naming the problem.

Vibe coding hits a ceiling — two failure modes that AI is unusually good at producing.

Brooks: "The hardest single part of building a software system is deciding precisely what to build." AI agents are compliant — they produce something for any prompt. That compliance is the problem.

A bare prompt produces well-tested scaffolding for a product no real user can use.

Tell the AI you are "an expert TypeScript programmer" and it draws on a broad blend of what "expert TypeScript" means across its training — common practice, not the best-defined practice. The output is competent but undifferentiated.

Without grounding in user outcomes, the AI builds confidently in the wrong direction.

The product might be technically sound and pass all tests — and still be useless. This is the failure no automated check catches.

AI-generated code contains security vulnerabilities at rates around 40%.

Pearce et al. (2022)

Ungrounded output is hallucination-prone, sloppy on edge cases, and weak on architectural consistency. The AI builds something fluently — and it looks right until you look carefully.

The 40% figure is from Pearce et al. (2022) — verified in the whitepaper. The Spracklen et al. (2024) stat on hallucinated package recommendations (21.7%) is also sourced there. Use both if time permits.

The AI does not know what "good" looks like — because it has no framework for evaluating quality.

Both failure modes pass casual inspection. You cannot find problems you have no framework to name. The missing ingredient is not more AI — it is the right published standards, activated at the right moment.

This sets up the contrast slide. The problem is not capability — it is the absence of a framework for quality. That is what GVM provides.

Name the expert who defined what good is.

This is the pivot. Everything before is the problem. Everything after is what becomes possible when you ground the AI in named experts and their published works.

A citation acts as a retrieval key — it activates a specific framework rather than the general blend.

When a prompt names a specific author and a specific work — "Apply Beizer's boundary value analysis from Software Testing Techniques" — the model's attention mechanism activates representations associated with Beizer's specific formulation, not a blend of every testing source.

Without the citation: a three-value test (min, max, middle) from an undifferentiated blend.

With the citation: the six-value boundary test with on-point and off-point distinction — Beizer's framework, applied with its full depth.

The retrieval-key metaphor is the right frame. The mechanism is analogous to how a search engine returns more focused results for "Knuth stable sort" than for "how to sort."

One sentence activates a body of work that would take thousands of words to specify explicitly.

The compression benefit applies regardless of the practitioner's own expertise. Even experts who know Tufte's work still face a transfer problem: decomposing it into explicit instructions every time. Citation-based activation eliminates that cost.

Panels of 3–7 competing experts produce better output than any single expert alone.

Consider the architecture phase. GVM assembles a panel where Fairbanks says "spec only the risky parts" and Brooks says "maintain conceptual integrity across everything." The tension is productive — it forces a principled balance for each project rather than defaulting to either extreme.

The result — a brief spec that connects to the whole — is better than either expert would produce alone. This is not additive; it is a genuine trade-off where competing priorities force a principled compromise.

The key insight: experts are not interchangeable decorations. Their frameworks genuinely conflict in useful ways. The panel's job is to force the resolution, not to agree with each other.

Expertise activates in three tiers — always-on process experts, conditional domain specialists, and stack specialists.

Not every expert is needed for every task. Conditional activation keeps context focused and avoids loading expertise the project doesn't need. The key property of Tier 2b: it gets more valuable the longer you use GVM in a domain.

A phased pipeline activates the right experts at each stage — from impact map to release.

The pipeline is not a waterfall. Feedback arrows go upstream. Test-case generation reveals untestable requirements; specification writing reveals missing tests. That feedback is the methodology working as designed.

Discovery phases decide what is worth building — before a line of architecture exists.

/gvm-impact-map produces a four-level goal → actor → impact → deliverable tree (Adzic). No requirement may proceed unless it traces to a leaf deliverable.

/gvm-requirements elicits, structures, and prioritises through Wiegers, Gause & Weinberg, Patton, Cooper, and Christensen/Moesta — five experts whose frameworks force completeness and testability.

/gvm-test-cases is a quality gate on requirements, not a downstream activity: before any architecture is designed, every requirement must be expressible as a concrete test.

The test-cases-before-spec ordering is the discipline. It forces requirement vagueness to surface before any code is written — cheapest point to fix it. AI agents are compliant: ask one to implement a vague requirement and it will produce something confidently, fluently, and wrong.

Test cases come before the technical spec — requirements must prove they are testable.

The test-cases phase produces a health report that identifies untestable, inconsistent, and contradictory requirements. These are resolved or explicitly acknowledged before the pipeline proceeds.

By the time the tech spec begins, every requirement has been validated through testability. The spec inherits clean inputs — not vague aspirations.

This matters specifically for AI-assisted development because LLMs are compliant — they will implement a vague requirement with full confidence. The test-first gate catches vagueness at zero cost, before any code exists.

MVP-1: the first build chunk must touch every external boundary — integration risk cannot accumulate silently.

Reinertsen: cost rises as the gap between "component complete" and "system integrated" widens. MVP-1 closes that gap on day one. The walking skeleton (/gvm-walking-skeleton) is the optional mechanical enabler: it registers every boundary before chunk planning begins.

A single reviewer misses what multiple non-overlapping panels catch — four reviews run in the wall-clock time of one.

The panels run as concurrent subagents. Each panel applies a liberal detection threshold on the first pass — maximise recall, filter false positives in synthesis. The diagnosing expert's framework defines what "right" looks like for the fix, not just what's wrong.

Without memory, every review starts from zero — progressive calibration fixes this.

By round 3, the reviewer knows: "The auth module scores 9 on security. The search module scored 5 in rounds 1 and 2. Input validation has been flagged twice." Reviews converge toward the project's actual quality profile.

When a finding recurs across three or more consecutive rounds, it is promoted to a build check — a concrete instruction loaded into subsequent build prompts. The system learns from its own review history.

Fagan: inspection processes should improve from inspection data. Deming: common-cause variation requires a process change, not just an output fix. Build checks are the methodology's memory of past defects.

The release gate makes review verdicts structural — not advisory.

The release phase aggregates every review verdict — code review, document review, design review — and blocks the release if any produced a "not ready" verdict.

Ship-ready — all reviews cleared, no open critical findings, documentation accurate.

Demo-ready — MUST requirements met, stubs acceptable if non-surface-facing.

Not shippable — open critical findings, or stubs that surface to the user.

A pipeline that produces verdicts but does not enforce them is a pipeline whose reviews are optional in practice.

This is the structural difference between a quality system and a quality aspiration. The release verdict also checks documentation accuracy — if the README describes features that were descoped, the tag is blocked until the documentation matches reality.

The evidence: grounding produces measurably better output.

Second pivot point — from methodology to evidence. The experiment is the load-bearing claim.

180 dashboards, 1,080 blind reviews, three model families, two independent review instruments.

ConditionDescription
Group 1 — UngroundedCore task only, no expert citations
Group 2 — Design-groundedTufte and Few principles cited
Group 3 — Fully groundedDesign + domain experts (Jorion, Hull) + writing experts (Doumont, Zinsser)

Each of 60 dashboards per model reviewed by 3 independent reviewers per instrument, with no reviewer seeing more than one dashboard. Reproduced across Claude Opus 4.6, GPT-5.4, and Gemini 3.1 Pro.

Experiment A used 8 dimensions derived from ungrounded AI consensus. Experiment B used 11 dimensions derived from the cited experts' published principles, confirmed by a human before use. The instruments are the key — same dashboards, different evaluation frameworks.

The group ranking reversed between experiments — the improvements were real but invisible without the framework to see them.

This is the core finding. The ranking reversal is not a model artifact — it reproduced identically across Claude, GPT, and Gemini. Ungrounded reviewers could not see the quality improvement. Expert-grounded reviewers could. Same dashboards, same reviewers, different evaluation framework.

The improvements were not marginal — effect sizes were exceptional.

DimensionCohen's d
Chartjunk absence3.67
Data-ink ratio3.87
Chart type appropriateness2.27

Cohen's d = 0.8 is conventionally "large." Values above 2 are exceptional. These improvements existed in the dashboards — they were just invisible to reviewers without the expert framework to name them.

Effect sizes above 2 are very rare in behavioural research. The effect was there all along — the reviewers just didn't have the framework to see it. This demonstrates both the quality effect of grounding and the measurement problem: ungrounded evaluation cannot detect grounded quality.

Ungrounded evaluation cannot detect grounded quality — you need the framework to see the improvement.

Experiment A reviewers could not distinguish Group 2's Tufte/Few-aligned design from Group 1's ungrounded output. The improvements were real — confirmed by expert reviewers under the grounded instrument — but invisible without the framework to evaluate them.

This is the core claim made concrete: the experts' published works are the definition of good. Only reviewers who know those standards can see the convergence.

The implication for teams: if you are evaluating AI-generated output without expert frameworks, you will not see quality differences that exist. The evaluation instrument matters as much as the generation method.

The experiment establishes the quality effect on one task type — wider claims are supported but not proven by it.

  • Same-model evaluation: reviewers were models from the same family. Cross-model replication is open work.
  • Task type: market risk dashboards. Generalisation requires replication across domains.
  • Discovery axis (impact mapping, exploratory testing effectiveness) requires separate experimental validation.
  • Head-to-head comparison against Role Prompting, SDD, BMAD, or RAG has not been run.
The whitepaper is explicit about these limits. Present them directly — they are a sign of intellectual honesty, not weakness. The experimental claim is narrow and solid; the wider claims are the paper's informed working position.

A real project: Assay was built by GVM, then used to run the experiment that validated GVM.

The meta-structure here matters: the tool that validates the methodology was itself built by the methodology. If GVM produces sloppy software, the experiment it ran would be invalid. The case study and the experiment corroborate each other.

Assay shipped in 35 hours: 38 requirements, 207 tests passing, site survey score 5.0/5.

MetricValue
Requirements implemented38 (all MUST met)

The site survey — an independent assessment of codebase health across coherence, currency, testability, modularity, documentation, and dependency health — scored 5.0 in every dimension. The assessment noted "one consistent pattern throughout." That consistency is not an accident of the code — it is a consequence of the methodology.

Building Assay with GVM taught three things the methodology had not anticipated.

  • Review findings do not converge linearly. Round 5 findings spiked from 19 to 30 because the streaming implementation introduced new code paths. This is the process working, not failing.
  • The build improved its own architecture. Duplicate dispatch logic was flagged as a recurring finding, promoted to build check BC-002, and extracted into a shared Dispatcher class — 150 lines eliminated. The review system drove the architectural improvement; upfront design had not.
  • Mocked tests pass with hallucinated code. The Anthropic streaming hallucination passed all unit tests because the mock accepted whatever call signature it received. Mocked tests verify logic, not integration.
These are honest findings from the case study — not cherry-picked success stories. The build-check mechanism, the review-to-construction feedback loop, and the mock-vs-real distinction are all methodology decisions that Assay stress-tested.

The experts whose frameworks power this output see no compensation — GVM makes that visible for the first time.

AI models already contain the full body of work from the experts GVM names. Practitioners who previously bought books, attended workshops, and studied frameworks now have that knowledge through AI — often without any awareness of its provenance.

Every expert activation through GVM is logged: loaded (read into context) and cited (named as the source of a decision). The log is the technical precondition for any compensation scheme.

Longpre et al. (2024) audited 1,800+ training datasets and found licence metadata absent or under-specified for over 70% of audited datasets. GVM makes attribution visible — necessary for compensation, though not sufficient. The remaining steps are tractable engineering once the log exists.

You can start with one citation — the full pipeline is not required.

"Apply Kleppmann's cache-aside pattern with TTLs calibrated to data volatility" is a single sentence that activates a body of work whether or not you are running the full GVM pipeline.

The methodology scales down as well as up. For throwaway scripts and rapid prototypes, the pipeline structure may exceed its benefit. For production systems, regulated industries, and software people depend on, the setup cost is justified when the alternative is shipping code grounded in an undifferentiated blend of training data.

The entry point is wherever the practitioner is. A single citation is better than none. The full pipeline is for when quality matters enough to warrant the overhead — and once established, the per-project cost drops sharply.

The full pipeline is low-ceremony to start — and gets cheaper with each project in the same domain.

The setup cost is front-loaded: curating reference files, assembling expert panels, establishing the pipeline structure. But once established, the per-project cost drops because the same reference files, pipeline structure, and core expert panels serve every subsequent project in that domain.

GVM is a methodology, not a product — the components travel as structured text, not as tooling.

The reference implementation uses Claude Code, but the methodology is portable: expert panels, curated reference files, phased pipelines, and conditional activation are patterns implementable in any AI development environment that supports contextual instructions.

Directly portable: expert reference files (Markdown), pipeline-phase definitions, scoring rubrics, build-check format.

Requires per-tool adaptation: conditional activation mechanism, parallel panel dispatch, calibration file location.

Portability follows from the foundation: text files containing curated expert knowledge, with citations that activate knowledge already present in any sufficiently capable LLM. The tool provides the loading mechanism; the text provides the grounding.

The plugin installs the full skill set. /gvm-status confirms the installation and shows which phases are available. /gvm-requirements is the entry point for most greenfield projects. /gvm-site-survey is the entry point for existing codebases.

Keep the vibes — but ground them in the published canon.

The published authorities in requirements, testing, architecture, security, and domain modelling are present in every capable LLM. They are already there. GVM activates them through citation, applies them through structured phases, and evaluates output against their frameworks — not against an undifferentiated blend of "reasonable practice."

The name is a deliberate riff on vibe coding. What it offers is what vibe coding alone cannot: the right experts, at the right phase, with the right framework for evaluating whether what was built is actually good.

The closing sentence is Duarte's "new bliss" — not a utopian promise but a specific, achievable transformation. The audience is the hero: they already have the AI capability; GVM is what makes it produce principled, evaluable output.
