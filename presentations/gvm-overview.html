Grounded Vibe Methodology

Named experts. Phased pipeline. Measurably better output.

May 2026  ·  30 minutes

Welcome. 30 minutes. Practitioners and leaders in the room — I'll flag when content is directed at one group specifically. By the end: practitioners know where to start; leaders know what adoption costs and what it returns.

Vibe coding produces plausible output — not necessarily correct output.

Ask an AI to build something with no further guidance. It compiles. The obvious tests pass. The PR looks fine on a fast read.

The failures show up in production: an edge case the model didn't consider, a security assumption that was wrong, an integration boundary that wasn't wired correctly.

These failures are not random. They cluster in predictable defect classes.

This is the inciting incident. The audience already knows this at some level. The question is whether they know it well enough to act on it.

AI codegen hits two failure modes that casual inspection cannot catch.

Failure mode 1

Building the wrong thing confidently. AI agents are compliant — they produce something for any prompt. Without grounding in what is worth building, they fluently build products no real user can use.

Failure mode 2

Building the right thing sloppily. Without grounding in what good looks like for architecture, testing, or security, the model draws from an undifferentiated blend of all the code it was trained on.

Brooks named failure mode 1: "the hardest single part of building a software system is deciding precisely what to build." Beck, Fowler, and the testing canon address failure mode 2. GVM addresses both.

Telling the AI it's "an expert engineer" isn't the same as telling it which expert to follow.

Ungrounded

"Implement caching for the data layer."

Produces a reasonable implementation. No framework for reasoning about consistency vs. staleness, or how long different data types should live in cache.

Grounded

"Implement caching per Kleppmann's cache-aside pattern with TTLs calibrated to data volatility."

Activates Kleppmann's framework. TTLs differ by data type. Invalidation handles eventual consistency. Cache strategy matches the read/write ratio.

Same prompting effort. Noticeably deeper output. The citation is the difference — it acts as a retrieval key into the model's training data, activating a focused framework rather than a blend.

The Grounded Vibe Methodology names the experts and builds the discipline around them.

Keep what works about vibe coding — natural language, conversational flow, low barrier to entry. Anchor every phase to named experts and their published works.

Citations act as retrieval keys: one sentence activates an expert's entire body of work, already encoded in the model's training data.

The turning point in the Duarte sparkline. We've established "what is." Now we move to "what could be." GVM is the answer to both failure modes.

One sentence compresses thousands of pages of published expert knowledge.

"Apply Tufte and Few's principles when designing this dashboard."

This single sentence activates: data-ink ratio maximisation, chartjunk elimination, chart type selection, small multiples, sparklines, table design, right-aligned numbers, semantic colour use — drawn from hundreds of pages across multiple books.

The expert wrote the book. The model trained on it. You activate it with a citation.

This is the core mechanism. GVM codifies this into reference files — structured, phase-keyed extracts that maximise the activation effect. The practitioner doesn't need to know all of Tufte; the reference file holds the relevant principles.

GVM organises experts into three tiers that activate at different scopes.

Tier 1 — Process experts (always active) Requirements: Wiegers, Gause & Weinberg, Patton  ·  Testing: Copeland, Beizer, North  ·  Architecture: Clements, Fairbanks, Brooks
Tier 2 — Domain specialists (conditional on signal) 2a Software: Kleppmann (data), Tufte & Few (dashboards), Cooper & Krug (UX), Nygard (resilience)  ·  2b Industry: Hull & Jorion (market risk), Altman (credit), domain-specific authorities
Tier 3 — Stack specialists (after technology choice) Python: Ramalho, Percival  ·  TypeScript: Vanderkam, Abramov  ·  SQL: Winand, Ben-Gan  ·  Infra: Majors, Hightower
The three tiers answer "which experts when." Tier 1 is always present for process. Tier 2 activates based on what the work involves. Tier 3 activates after the tech stack is chosen. Conditional activation keeps context focused — not every expert is loaded for every task.

Panels of competing experts force principled trade-offs that single experts cannot.

Architecture panel example: Fairbanks says "spec only the risky parts." Brooks says "maintain conceptual integrity everywhere." The tension forces a principled compromise — a brief spec (Fairbanks wins on depth) that cross-references system conventions (Brooks wins on consistency).

The result is better than either expert would produce alone. This is not additive — it is a genuine trade-off where competing priorities produce a reasoned resolution.

GVM assembles panels of 3-7 experts per discipline. The expert panel for a given phase is in the reference files and is shown transparently to the practitioner — they can see who is active and why.

The pipeline activates the right experts at each of fourteen phases.

Discovery → Construction → Verification → Release. Feedback arrows allow upstream revision at every stage.

The four groups match the two failure modes plus quality assurance and delivery. Discovery answers "what is worth building." Construction builds it correctly. Verification confirms both. Release gates against both verdicts.

Test cases come before architecture — if a requirement can't be tested, it's ambiguous.

/gvm-test-cases runs after requirements and before the tech spec. Generating test cases forces every requirement to be expressed as a concrete, observable outcome. Ambiguous requirements fail this gate — and it is cheaper to fix them now than after the spec is written.

Teams that normally write tests after code find this backwards. That is the point.

This is the single structural change most likely to surprise practitioners. Ground it in Copeland and Beizer — they established this as best practice. BDD format (North) makes the tests readable by non-technical stakeholders, which also helps the leaders in the room.

Review findings are scored and the calibration history persists across rounds.

Each /gvm-code-review round dispatches parallel expert panels — each scanning a specific defect class: contracts and interfaces, logic and completeness, security, naming and spec compliance.

Findings are scored. The verdict is recorded in reviews/calibration.md. By round three, the reviewer knows which modules have recurring findings and which have clean histories. Reviews get more precise over time — not because the AI gets smarter, but because the calibration file gives it project-specific context it would otherwise lack.

The calibration file is the audit trail. It's how the methodology improves across a project. It's also what the leader checks to verify the gate is being enforced — not bypassed.

Every finding is triaged by the practitioner — nothing is silently filtered or deferred.

GVM's shared rule 28: every Critical and Important finding is presented to the practitioner before any disposition decision. Options: fix now, defer with documented rationale, dismiss as false positive with reasoning.

The verdict is not recorded until all findings are triaged. The calibration file is the audit trail. If findings are marked N/A without rationale, the gate is failing silently.

This is process discipline, not tooling. The plugin enforces the workflow; the team enforces the discipline. Leaders: this is what you check in the calibration file.

Defect patterns that recur across review rounds are promoted to automated build checks.

A finding that surfaces three consecutive times in review rounds signals a systemic pattern, not a one-off oversight. GVM promotes recurring findings to build checks — concrete instructions that run automatically on every subsequent build.

This converts a discovered defect pattern into a prevention mechanism. The methodology improves with use, not just with expert loading.

Deming: fix the process, not the output repeatedly. Fagan's inspection research showed that checklists should evolve from prior findings. GVM implements this mechanically.

What the data shows

Grounding produces measurably better output — and ungrounded evaluation cannot see the difference.

Transition to the evidence section. This is where we ground the "what could be" in empirical results rather than assertion.

The experiment: 180 dashboards, 1,080 blind reviews, three AI model families.

Grounding condition What was included Dashboards
Group 1 — ungrounded Task prompt only 60 (20 per model)
Group 2 — design-grounded Task + Tufte & Few principles 60
Group 3 — fully grounded Task + design + domain + writing experts 60
Total blind reviews (2 review conditions × 3 reviewers × 180) 1,080

Replicated across Claude Opus 4.6, Gemini 3.1 Pro, and GPT-5.4. Dashboards shuffled and stripped of grounding identifiers before review. (Source: Brennan & Quinn, Grounding Produces Measurably Better Output, April 2026.)

Be honest about scope: one task type (market risk dashboards), one project. No control group in the traditional sense. The honest claim is dose-response replication across three model families — not generalisability across all task types.

Expert review flips the group ranking in all three models — ungrounded review cannot see the quality.

Model Ungrounded review ranking Expert review ranking
Claude Opus 4.6 G2 < G1 < G3 G1 < G2 < G3 ✓
Gemini 3.1 Pro G2 < G3 < G1 G1 < G2 < G3 ✓
GPT-5.4 G2 < G1 < G3 G1 < G2 < G3 ✓

Under expert review: G1 < G2 < G3 in all three models (p < 0.001). The dose-response hypothesis holds. Under ungrounded review: two models show design grounding appearing to make things worse. (Source: Brennan & Quinn, 2026.)

The central finding: the quality is in the dashboards. The ability to see it is in the reviewer. You need expert grounding on both sides — production and evaluation.

Message-driven structure — Doumont's principle — produces the largest single effect in all three models.

Ungrounded / design-only (G1, G2)

Panel titles are category labels:

"VaR Utilisation"  ·  "Stress Testing"

Score: 4.9–5.7 out of 10

Fully grounded (G3)

Panel titles convey analytical conclusions:

"Two desks are near breach"  ·  "2008-style spread widening remains the worst-case scenario"

Score: 8.95–9.90 out of 10

Effect size (G1 vs G3): d = −10.70 (Claude), −7.44 (Gemini), −16.19 (GPT-5.4). (Source: Brennan & Quinn, 2026.)

This is the Doumont effect — structure by message, not by topic. Neither G1 nor G2 received Doumont's guidance. The improvement is specific to the grounding content, not a general benefit of longer prompts.

What the evidence does and does not show.

  • It shows: dose-response grounding effect replicated across three independent model families (p < 0.001)
  • It shows: ungrounded review cannot detect grounded quality improvements
  • It does not show: generalisation across all task types — one domain (market risk dashboards), one team
  • It does not show: cross-model replication under grounded review — same-model evaluation only
  • Open work: cross-model replication, different task domains, longer-term outcome metrics
Orwell's rule: never say more than you know to be true. The experiment is real and the results are significant, but the scope is limited. Honest about what the evidence claims.

For practitioners

How to start using GVM on your next project.

Directing content at practitioners specifically. Leaders: this section tells you what the team will experience day-to-day.

Start with /gvm-doc-review on an internal document — not with the full build pipeline.

Run the doc-review gate on a status report, a technical design, or a strategy document. The review discipline you learn there — expert panels, scored findings, triage decisions — is identical to what /gvm-code-review applies to code.

Four to six weeks of doc reviews teaches the methodology without touching production code paths. If the findings are catching real problems, the case for extending to code is self-evident.

Recommended starting point from the Engineering Leader's Guide. Low-stakes context, bounded scope, immediate feedback on whether the gate produces signal.

The first three review rounds build calibration — treat scores as decision-grade only after that.

A score of 7.5 on a first-pass review means something different from 7.5 on round four of a calibrated review. The calibration file accumulates project-specific anchors, resolved-finding records, and recurring pattern history. The numbers look the same; the information content is not.

Three calibrated reviews before treating any score as a go/no-go input. This is a hard minimum, not a guideline.

Calibration is one of the things practitioners most commonly skip. The calibration training document covers this in detail. Route them there before they use review scores for release decisions.

Every phase produces a traceable artefact that the next phase consumes.

The pipeline is not bureaucracy — it is traceability. Every decision is auditable, and every finding traces to the expert principle that identified it.

Practitioners often ask "why so many artefacts." The answer is that each artefact is an input to the next phase, not just documentation of what happened. The requirements feed test-cases which feed build which feeds code-review which feeds deploy.

For engineering leaders

What adopting GVM means for your team — and what it costs.

Directing content at leaders specifically. Practitioners: this section tells you how leadership will think about what you're doing.

Installing the plugin takes two minutes. Adopting GVM takes weeks — and they are not the same thing.

GVM makes structured review a quality gate the team enforces on every meaningful change, not a step they skip when the deadline presses. If your team is already doing that, GVM gives it structure and traceability. If they aren't, GVM requires a process change — and that's the thing the plugin cannot do for you.

The key question: is your team currently reading AI-generated code carefully before merging it? If yes, GVM structures and amplifies that practice. If no, the problem isn't tooling.
From the Engineering Leader's Guide. This is the most important thing for leaders to understand. The plugin is the reference implementation; the team's process is what enforces the quality gate.

GVM costs time — the right question is whether the time is worth it.

CostTypical scopeReturn
Review rounds per substantive change1–2 rounds to passing verdict; 4 rounds for public-facing documents (Brennan & Quinn whitepaper)Findings that catch things before production

The cost of a review round is bounded. The cost of a production regression is not.

Do not let anyone tell you GVM is free. The right question is cost vs. regression rate. For teams shipping fast with acceptable regression rates, the calculation looks different than for teams with high rework rates.

Measure things that tell you whether the quality gate is working — not how busy the team is.

MetricAction threshold
Calibrated vs blind score gapGap > 1.5 points: check calibration file for staleness
Finding count by expert panelAny panel averaging > 2 Critical findings per round: schedule targeted improvement
Review rounds to passing verdictAverage > 3 rounds: look upstream at test cases and spec quality, not at reviews
Zero rework on 3 consecutive reviewsAudit the calibration file — this may indicate silent suppression

Do not measure: reviewer count, AI tokens spent, number of phases completed. These are activity metrics.

The calibration file is the audit trail. Check it monthly. If you can't find evidence that findings are being triaged with rationale, the gate is failing silently.

Three adoption paths — all legitimate, depending on your risk tolerance.

  • Option 1 — Standalone first (recommended). Run /gvm-doc-review only. Team learns the review discipline on documents before committing to it on code. Four to six weeks on internal documents. If findings are catching real problems, the case extends itself.
  • Option 2 — Toe in water. One team, one greenfield project, full pipeline. Six to ten weeks. Protect the project scope explicitly — deadline pressure will make the pipeline the first thing cut.
  • Option 3 — Pipeline by phase. Requirements and test cases first. Add build and code review once the team trusts the upstream artefacts. Slowest but stickiest — teams that adopt phase-by-phase own the methodology more deeply.
The Engineering Leader's Guide has detailed guidance for each path, including exercises and failure modes to watch for. Direct leaders there after this session.

Three decisions that cannot be delegated to the team.

  • The triage call on Critical findings under deadline pressure. Write down your policy before the first deadline conflict. What happens when a release has a Critical finding and a real deadline? "Defer with rationale" is acceptable. Decide now, not under pressure.
  • The expert roster for your primary domain. The default roster covers general software development. If your team works in a specialised domain, run /gvm-init and verify the discovered experts are genuinely authoritative in your domain.
  • The branding configuration. One session at onboarding. If you leave it unset, document quality drifts across the team.
These are leadership decisions because they set constraints the team works within for months. They cannot be delegated. The first one — the deadline-pressure policy — is the most important.

This is not circular — it is dogfooding. The methodology was applied to a real project with real constraints and a real deadline. The case study is transparent about where the methodology helped and where it was costly.

Silent suppression combined with deadline pressure is how the methodology fails in practice.

  • Silent suppression: findings marked N/A without rationale. Check the calibration file's N/A entries. No rationale column = gate not enforced.
  • Small-change bypass: "this change is too small to need review." Define the threshold up-front and hold it.
  • Skipping calibration: using review scores as decision inputs before three calibrated rounds. The numbers look real; the information content isn't yet.

Each failure mode is individually manageable. The combination — all three together under deadline pressure — unwinds the methodology quickly. The calibration file is the early warning system.

These are documented failure modes from real deployments. Leaders: watch the calibration file monthly. Practitioners: if you're marking things N/A under pressure without rationale, flag it rather than proceed.

GVM in two sentences.

GVM is a phased development methodology that anchors every phase to named experts and their published works — activating focused bodies of knowledge from the model's training data through citation rather than persona description.

The pipeline addresses both failure modes of AI codegen: deciding what is worth building (discovery phases grounded in Adzic, Cagan, Wiegers) and building it correctly (construction and verification phases grounded in Beck, Fowler, Fagan, and the testing and architecture canon).
