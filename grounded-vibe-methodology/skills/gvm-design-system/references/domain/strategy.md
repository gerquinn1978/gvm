## Strategy

### Richard Rumelt

**Source:**
- Richard Rumelt, *Good Strategy Bad Strategy: The Difference and Why It Matters*, Crown Business (2011)
- Richard Rumelt, *The Crux: How Leaders Become Strategists*, Currency (2022)

**Expert score:**
| Authority | Publication | Breadth | Adoption | Currency | Classification |
|-----------|-------------|---------|----------|----------|----------------|
| 5 | 5 | 4 | 5 | 5 | **Canonical** |

Evidence: Authority — Harry and Elsa Kunin Chair Emeritus at UCLA Anderson; Harvard Business School doctorate (1972); founding member and president of the Strategic Management Society (1995-98). McKinsey Quarterly: "strategy's strategist." Publication — *Good Strategy Bad Strategy* FT & Goldman Sachs Business Book of Year finalist; *The Crux* selected by FT and Globe & Mail as top business book. Breadth — resource-based view of strategy, competitive dynamics, corporate diversification. His 1991 paper "How Much Does Industry Matter?" is one of the most cited strategy papers. Currency=5: *The Crux* (2022) is recent and Rumelt publishes actively in McKinsey Quarterly.

**Work scores:**
| Work | Specificity | Depth | Currency | Influence | Classification |
|------|-------------|-------|----------|-----------|----------------|
| *Good Strategy Bad Strategy* | 4 | 5 | 4 | 5 | **Canonical** |
| *The Crux* | 4 | 4 | 5 | 4 | **Established** |

Evidence: *GSBS* Influence — "diagnosis → guiding policy → coherent actions" became a widely adopted strategy articulation framework. *The Crux* Currency — 2022, addresses how to identify the critical challenge.


**Activation signals:** strategy documents, strategic plans, competitive analysis, strategic diagnosis, guiding policy, coherent actions

**Key principles:**
- **The kernel of good strategy** — diagnosis (what's going on?), guiding policy (the approach to dealing with it), and coherent actions (coordinated steps that implement the policy). A strategy that lacks any of these three elements is incomplete.
- **Bad strategy is not the absence of good strategy** — it's a distinct pattern: fluff (buzzwords substituting for thought), failure to face the challenge, mistaking goals for strategy, and bad strategic objectives.
- **The crux** — the most critical challenge that must be addressed. Good strategists identify the crux and focus resources on it rather than spreading effort across everything.
- **Coherence** — the actions must reinforce each other. A strategy where the actions pull in different directions is not a strategy; it's a list.

### A.G. Lafley & Roger Martin

**Source:** A.G. Lafley & Roger Martin, *Playing to Win: How Strategy Really Works*, Harvard Business Review Press (2013)

**Expert score:**
| Authority | Publication | Breadth | Adoption | Currency | Classification |
|-----------|-------------|---------|----------|----------|----------------|
| 5 | 5 | 4 | 5 | 4 | **Canonical** |

Evidence: Authority — Lafley: two-time P&G CEO (2000-2010, 2013-2015), doubled P&G sales, quadrupled profits; CEO of the Year 2006. Martin: Dean of Rotman School of Management (1998-2013), Thinkers50 #1 management thinker (2017). Publication — WSJ and Washington Post bestseller; Thinkers50 Best Book 2012-13. Publication=5: WSJ and Washington Post bestseller; Thinkers50 Best Book 2012-13; multiple bestseller lists and awards. Adoption — five cascading choices framework widely adopted in corporate strategy. Currency=4: framework actively taught and cited in post-2018 strategy literature; Martin continues publishing and speaking.

**Work score — *Playing to Win*:**
| Specificity | Depth | Currency | Influence | Classification |
|-------------|-------|----------|-----------|----------------|
| 5 | 4 | 4 | 5 | **Canonical** |

Evidence: Specificity — concrete framework for strategy operationalisation with real P&G examples. Depth — covers all five choices with worked examples. Influence — the five cascading choices framework is taught at most major business schools and used by Fortune 500 companies.


**Activation signals:** strategy operationalisation, strategic choices, where-to-play, how-to-win, strategy cascade, competitive advantage

**Key principles:**
- **Five cascading choices** — (1) What is our winning aspiration? (2) Where will we play? (3) How will we win? (4) What capabilities must be in place? (5) What management systems are required? Each choice constrains the next.
- **Strategy is choice** — a strategy that tries to serve all customers in all markets with all products is not a strategy. Strategy means deliberately choosing what not to do.
- **Where-to-play before how-to-win** — choose the playing field (market, segment, geography, channel) before deciding the competitive approach. The playing field determines what "winning" looks like.
- **Capabilities must match the strategy** — if the strategy requires capabilities the organisation doesn't have, either build them, acquire them, or change the strategy. A strategy that assumes capabilities that don't exist is wishful thinking.
