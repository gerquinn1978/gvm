## User Documentation & UX Writing

### Mark Baker

**Source:** Mark Baker, *Every Page is Page One: Topic-Based Writing for Technical Communication and the Web*, XML Press (2013)

**Expert score:**
| Authority | Publication | Breadth | Adoption | Currency | Classification |
|-----------|-------------|---------|----------|----------|----------------|
| 4 | 3 | 3 | 4 | 2 | **Recognised** |

Evidence: Authority — 25+ years in structured authoring, SGML 95 presenter, coined "EPPO" concept adopted across tech comm community. Adoption — "every page is page one" entered common vocabulary in technical writing.

**Work score — *Every Page is Page One*:**
| Specificity | Depth | Currency | Influence | Classification |
|-------------|-------|----------|-----------|----------------|
| 4 | 4 | 3 | 4 | **Established** |

Evidence: Specificity — precisely targeted at topic-based web documentation with named EPPO framework. Influence — concept cited routinely in TC discussions; idratherbewriting.com calls it a "technical-communication classic."


**Activation signals:** user documentation, help systems, knowledge bases, in-app guidance, technical communication, topic-based writing

**Key principles:**
- **Every page is page one** — users arrive via search, not via a table of contents. Every help page must be self-contained and provide enough context to be useful without reading anything else.
- **Topic-based architecture** — organise documentation by topic (one concept per page), not by book structure (chapters, sequential reading order). Users forage for answers; they don't read linearly.
- **Rich linking over sequential structure** — connect topics through contextual links, not "next page" navigation. The reader constructs their own path through the content.
- **Qualified by context** — each topic states what it applies to and what the prerequisites are. A user landing on a topic knows immediately whether it's relevant to their situation.

### Torrey Podmajersky

**Source:** Torrey Podmajersky, *Strategic Writing for UX: Drive Engagement, Conversion, and Retention with Every Word*, O'Reilly (2019)

**Expert score:**
| Authority | Publication | Breadth | Adoption | Currency | Classification |
|-----------|-------------|---------|----------|----------|----------------|
| 4 | 4 | 3 | 4 | 4 | **Established** |

Evidence: Publication — O'Reilly, IEEE Xplore review, 2nd edition 2025. Adoption — translated into 5 languages, teaches at UW iSchool, routinely on recommended reading lists. Currency — 2nd edition updated for AI-generated UX content, actively consulting and teaching.

**Work score — *Strategic Writing for UX*:**
| Specificity | Depth | Currency | Influence | Classification |
|-------------|-------|----------|-----------|----------------|
| 4 | 3 | 4 | 4 | **Established** |

Evidence: Specificity — concrete UX text patterns (buttons, errors, empty states), voice framework, content audit methods. Currency — 2nd edition 2025 updated every chapter for AI and modern tools.


**Activation signals:** microcopy, UI text, button labels, error messages, empty states, onboarding copy, form instructions, confirmation dialogs, notification text

**Key principles:**
- **Voice and tone framework** — define the product's voice (consistent personality) and vary tone by context (celebratory for success, empathetic for errors, direct for actions). Document these as reusable patterns.
- **Microcopy serves the moment** — every piece of UI text exists to help the user complete a specific task at a specific moment. "Submit" is worse than "Place order" because it doesn't confirm what happens next.
- **Error messages diagnose and direct** — state what went wrong, why, and what the user can do about it. "Something went wrong" fails all three. "We couldn't save your changes because the file is too large (max 10MB). Try compressing the image." succeeds.
- **Empty states are onboarding** — a screen with no data is a teaching opportunity. "No projects yet" is a dead end. "No projects yet — create your first project to get started" is an affordance.
- **Consistency across surfaces** — the same action uses the same verb everywhere. If the nav says "Settings" and the button says "Preferences," that's a UX writing bug.
