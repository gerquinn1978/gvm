## Training & Instructional Design

### Ruth Colvin Clark & Richard E. Mayer

**Source:** Ruth Colvin Clark & Richard E. Mayer, *e-Learning and the Science of Instruction: Proven Guidelines for Consumers and Designers of Multimedia Learning* (5th ed.), Wiley (2024)

**Expert score:**
| Authority | Publication | Breadth | Adoption | Currency | Classification |

| 5 | 5 | 4 | 5 | 5 | **Canonical** |

Evidence: Authority — Clark: EdD (USC), past president of ISPI, Thomas F. Gilbert Distinguished Professional Achievement Award (2006), ASTD Legend Speaker (2007). Mayer: Professor of Psychology at UC Santa Barbara (since 1975), 390+ publications, 23 books, E.L. Thorndike Award (2000, the top award in educational psychology), James McKeen Cattell Fellow Award (2018). Publication — 5th edition 2024 from Wiley. Adoption — standard reference in instructional design programmes worldwide. Mayer's multimedia learning theory is one of the most cited frameworks in the field.

**Work score — *e-Learning and the Science of Instruction* (5th ed.):**
| Specificity | Depth | Currency | Influence | Classification |
|-------------|-------|----------|-----------|----------------|
| 5 | 5 | 5 | 5 | **Canonical** |

Evidence: Specificity — every chapter grounded in controlled experiments on multimedia learning. Depth — covers 15+ evidence-based principles with experimental backing. Currency — 5th edition 2024, 21 years of continuous updates. Influence — cited in virtually every instructional design textbook; Mayer's principles are industry standard.


**Activation signals:** training materials, e-learning, instructional design, learning objectives, multimedia learning, course content, educational materials, onboarding training

**Key principles:**
- **Multimedia principle** — people learn better from words and pictures together than from words alone. But the pictures must be relevant, not decorative.
- **Contiguity principle** — place related text and graphics near each other on the page (spatial contiguity) and present corresponding narration and animation simultaneously (temporal contiguity). Separation forces the learner to split attention.
- **Coherence principle** — remove extraneous material. Interesting-but-irrelevant stories, background music, and decorative graphics hurt learning. Every element must serve the learning objective.
- **Segmenting principle** — break complex lessons into learner-paced segments rather than presenting as a continuous unit. Learners need time to process before receiving new information.
- **Personalisation principle** — use conversational style ("you") rather than formal style ("the learner"). First and second person increase engagement and learning outcomes.

Currency — 2nd edition 2015; no recent update. Influence — strong practitioner adoption but narrower academic standing than Clark & Mayer.


**Activation signals:** training design, behaviour change, learner motivation, knowledge gaps, practice activities, learning retention, skill development

**Key principles:**
- **Identify the real gap** — before designing training, determine whether the problem is a knowledge gap, a skill gap, a motivation gap, or an environment gap. Training only fixes the first two. If people know what to do but don't do it, training is the wrong solution.
- **Design for the journey, not the destination** — learning is a process with stages: awareness → understanding → practice → application. Each stage needs different design approaches. Don't jump to assessment before the learner has had guided practice.
- **Practice is where learning happens** — information presentation is not learning. Learners need to do something with the information: apply it, make decisions with it, solve problems using it. Passive reading is the least effective learning method.
- **Make it sticky** — use stories, vivid examples, and emotional connections to make content memorable. Abstract principles are forgotten; concrete stories are remembered.
- **Respect the learner's time** — every minute of training should earn its place. If something can be provided as a job aid (reference card, checklist) instead of memorised, provide it as a job aid.
