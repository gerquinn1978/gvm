### Cliff Atkinson

**Source:** Cliff Atkinson, *Beyond Bullet Points: Using PowerPoint to Tell a Compelling Story That Gets Results* (4th ed.), Microsoft Press (2018)

| 5 | 4 | 4 | 3 | **Established** |

Evidence: Specificity — directly addresses presentation content structure and narrative flow. Depth — complete methodology from script to slide. Influence — solid but narrower than Duarte/Reynolds; primarily known for the story-first approach to slide decks.


**Activation signals:** presentation content, slide structure, presentation narrative, slide decks, keynotes, pitch decks

**Key principles:**
- **Story first, slides second** — write the narrative as a script before creating any slides. The story structure determines the slide structure, not the other way around.
- **Three-act structure for presentations** — Act I (setting + turning point), Act II (development + crisis), Act III (climax + resolution). Every presentation has a beginning, middle, and end.
- **One idea per slide** — each slide carries a single message. The headline states the point; the body provides evidence. No multi-point slides.
- **Headlines are sentences, not topics** — "Revenue increased 23% in Q3" is a headline. "Q3 Revenue" is a topic label. Headlines tell the story when read in sequence.

**Activation signals:** presentation narrative, persuasive presentations, visual storytelling, audience engagement, presentation arc, pitch decks, keynotes

**Key principles:**
- **Sparkline structure** — alternate between "what is" (current reality) and "what could be" (the vision). The tension between these creates forward momentum. End with the "new bliss" — the transformed future state.
- **The audience is the hero** — the presenter is the mentor, not the hero. Frame the content around the audience's journey and transformation, not the presenter's expertise.
- **Contrast creates meaning** — use visual contrast (big vs small, dark vs light, sparse vs dense) to direct attention and create hierarchy. Every slide should have a clear focal point.
- **Data slides need a clear "so what?"** — a chart without an insight is decoration. State the takeaway in the headline; design the visual to make that takeaway obvious.

| Specificity | Depth | Currency | Influence | Classification |
|-------------|-------|----------|-----------|----------------|
| 4 | 3 | 4 | 4 | **Established** |

Evidence: Specificity — focused on presentation visual simplicity and design. Depth — principles-based rather than procedural. Influence — popularised the "less is more" approach to slides; routinely paired with Duarte in presentation design curricula.


**Activation signals:** presentation visual design, slide simplicity, visual restraint, presentation aesthetics, slide imagery

**Key principles:**
- **Simplicity and restraint** — the best slides have very little on them. Remove elements until removing anything more would lose meaning. White space is not empty space — it's breathing room.
- **Signal-to-noise ratio** — every element on a slide either supports the message or distracts from it. Maximise signal, eliminate noise. This is Tufte's data-ink ratio applied to presentations.
- **Full-bleed images over clipart** — use high-quality, full-slide images that create emotional connection. Never use stock clipart, bullet-point templates, or decorative clip art.
- **Design is about restraint, not decoration** — a well-designed slide looks effortless. The design should be invisible — the content is what the audience notices.
